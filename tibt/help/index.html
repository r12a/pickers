List of character apps

Updated 11-Nov-2020

This Unicode character app allows you to produce or analyse runs of Tibetan text. character apps are especially useful for people who don't know a script well, as characters are displayed in ways that aid identification.

If something is broken or missing raise an issue. For version information see the Github commit list. The pictures of the UI used in this page are taken from a variety of character apps.

Inputting characters

The app includes characters in the Unicode Tibetan block that are used for writing text in the Lhasa Tibetan dialect.

To analyse text, copy & paste it into the large text area and select the desired control(s).

To produce Tibetan text in the text area, do one of the following:

  • Type on your keyboard, using type-assist. A quick way to input text from ASCII characters on your keyboard, based on a Latin transcription.
  • Click on the characters in the selection area. The blocks are arranged to assist with input, and groups of characters are arranged within a block phonetically.

The above are described in more detail elsewhere on this page.

Transliterate. Converts the contents of the text area to a Latin transliteration that provides a one-to-one correspondence between Tibetan and Latin symbols.

The transliteration scheme was developed for this character app, and, where possible, uses symbols that represent sounds typically associated with their Tibetan equivalents, rather than the sometimes fairly arbitrary selection of diacritics found in other systems. Of course, the complexities of mapping graphemes to sounds means that this is far from a real phonetic transcription, and it doesn't show vowels for unvowelled text, but the process is reversible (unlike most transcriptions).

Transliterations produced this way are particularly useful for understanding the composition of text in scripts, where the individual parts are not well separated in the text.

Translit+. Builds on the transliteration scheme just mentioned by

  • converting sequences representing foreign sounds to a single symbol
  • converting the tsek to a space.

This transcription may not be reversible. The aim is to produce a Latin-based version of the original text that is a little more readable than the transliteration.

Tibetan to Wylie. Transcribes the contents of the box below from Tibetan to latin script using the extended version of Wylie's transcription method as described by The Tibetan & Himalayan Library. By default, the whole content of the box is transcribed, but if you have selected a range of text only that range will be transcribed. The result appears in a separate area below the box.

Where ambiguity exists, and there is more than one way to transcribe a character, the displayed text will show alternatives. Click on the alternative that you prefer, and the others will disappear.

Note: I implemented most of the rules, but I used diacritics over lowercase letters rather than uppercase letters, except for the fixed form characters. I also didn't provide conversions for many of the symbols ‚Äì they will appear without change in the transcription. There may be some bugs for the less common aspects of the transcription, but the are not likely to occur for native tibetan words.

Wylie to Tibetan. Opens the THL's Online Tibetan Transliteration Converter, since Wylie's transcription doesn't make it easy to go from Latin to Tibetan, and I haven't yet implemented a converter myself.

Wylie to Tibetan. Allows you to generate Tibetan text from a THL Extended Wylie Transliteration Schema transcription. It works as described in the section just above about Reverse transliteration, but uses a different panel to set up the transcription type-in mode. It currently offers only a basic set of characters.
