List of character apps

Updated 31-Mar-2021

This Unicode character app allows you to produce or analyse runs of Modern Standard Arabic text. Character apps are especially useful for people who don't know a script well, as characters are displayed in ways that aid identification.

If something is broken or missing raise an issue. For version information see the Github commit list. The pictures of the UI used in this page are taken from a variety of character apps.

Inputting characters

The app includes characters in the Unicode Arabic block that are used for writing text in modern Standard Arabic. A number of regional forms are included, and coloured differently.

To analyse text, copy & paste it into the large text area and select the desired control(s).

To produce Adlam text in the text area, do one of the following:

  • Type on your keyboard, using type-assist. A quick way to input text from ASCII characters on your keyboard, based on a Latin transcription.
  • Open the shape-based input, and click on similar shapes to highlight characters you may be looking for. Good when you don't know the orthography well.
  • Click on the characters in the selection area. The blocks are arranged to assist with input, and groups of characters are arranged within a block phonetically.

The above are described in more detail below.

Click on FORMAT to access and input invisible formatting characters used to control direction, joining forms, etc.

Transliterate. Converts the contents of the text area to a Latin transliteration that provides a one-to-one correspondence between Arabic and Latin symbols.

The transliteration scheme was developed for this character app, and, where possible, uses symbols that represent sounds typically associated with their Arabic equivalents, rather than the sometimes fairly arbitrary selection of diacritics found in other systems. Of course, the complexities of mapping graphemes to sounds means that this is far from a real phonetic transcription, and it doesn't show vowels for unvowelled text, but the process is reversible (unlike most transcriptions).

Transliterations produced this way are particularly useful for understanding the composition of text in scripts, where the individual parts are not well separated in the text.

Make vocab. This helps speed up the creation of entries for the vocabulary app. You can create a vocab item with up to 5 parts, delimited by / or |, in the following order:

  1. native text
  2. meaning
  3. transcription (IPA or other, optional)
  4. alternative transcription (if needed)
  5. notes (optional)

Much of the time, only the first 2 items are needed. In this app if an IPA transcription is not supplied it will be added automatically, though it should be checked carefully.

The app will automatically generate an IPA transcription if one is not given, will remove vowels from the initial slot, and will copy the vowelled term to the alternative description slot with brackets around it.

Here is an example of before and after.

سَيَارَة/car

will produce:

سيارة|car|sajaːraʰ|(سَيَارَة)

The app will also, simultaneously, produce markup for examples in the secondary text area (see below).

If you have more than one line of text in the text area, you must highlight the line you want to convert. Only convert one line at a time.

Arabic to LOC. Produces a Library of Congress transcription of the text. To get a full transcription it's necessary to provide short vowel characters. If you don't provide any you will get a warning, but the transcription will still go ahead.

The transcription appears just below the input box, where you can copy it, move it into the input box at the caret, or delete it. It is generated using rules, rather than just by substitutions.

Arabic to ISO. Converts the contents of the text area to an ISO Latin transcription. It doesn't produce short vowels that aren't in the original text.

Input aids

Keys are mapped to characters in the Library of Congress transcription.
